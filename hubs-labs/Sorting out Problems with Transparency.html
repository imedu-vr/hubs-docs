Sorting out Problems with Transparency

May 31, 2022 ‚Äî 17 min read

Transparency can greatly enhance the look of a Hubs scene, but getting it to look right isn't always so clear.

Just when you thought you understood how to make cool scenes for Hubs, inevitably you run into a challenge when trying to make something look like glass or otherwise be partially see-through.

In order to know how to fix common problems with transparency, it helps to first understand the different types of transparency in computer graphics. So buckle up and hopefully this will make things more...transparent.

Alpha what?

The terms ‚ÄòAlpha Channel‚Äô, ‚ÄòImage Alpha‚Äô, or just ‚ÄòAlpha‚Äô are commonly used when talking about transparency. The good news is that they all refer to the same exact thing. Here‚Äôs a quick overview of some fundamentals in case you never learned anything about it. (shhh‚Ä¶ I won‚Äôt tell anyone you don‚Äôt know.)

Color images (on a computer‚Äìnot print, that‚Äôs a different topic, although not too different.) typically contain three layers of information, often referred to as ‚Äòchannels‚Äô. Those three channels are Red, Green, and Blue. Those three basic hues, with all their possible values (aka, brightness) and saturation (aka ‚Äòhow much color‚Äô) values, can create around 16 million colors when combined in different amounts. I‚Äôm not going to get deep into the topic of additive vs. subtractive color; the point is that those three color channels can do quite a bit, but they contain absolutely no information about transparency. This is where a fourth channel comes into play: The alpha channel

üí°
If you ever wondered why it‚Äôs called ‚Äòalpha‚Äô, it‚Äôs been explained by Alvy Ray Smith, cofounder of Lucasfilm‚Äôs Computer Division and Pixar who was one of its inventors:
‚ÄúWe called it that because of the classic linear interpolation formula Œ± A + ( 1 ‚àí Œ± ) B that uses the Greek letter Œ± (alpha) to control the amount of interpolation between, in this case, two images A and B."

The alpha channel, when viewed by itself, looks just like the R, G, & B channels, except that the brightness of each pixel determines just how transparent or opaque that part of the image should be. So a completely white pixel would be opaque whereas a completely black pixel would be invisible. Any values in-between are some amount of semi-transparency.

"Ok! Alpha = transparency. Got it. Let‚Äôs go!"

Not so fast there, friend. Not every file type can support an alpha channel. This is the first important thing to remember. So if you see a .JPG (jpeg), for example, you can automatically assume it does not have any transparency information.

For our purposes of making content for Hubs, we‚Äôre generally going to be using the .PNG file format for most things. There are tradeoffs for using one type of format over another and .PNG is no exception, but because it supports having transparency, it will serve us well for most materials and textures we will make.

ü§ì
Nerd trivia alert: .PNG got its original (dumb recursive) name more for what it isn‚Äôt than for what it is. It originally (unofficially) stood for ‚ÄúPNG‚Äôs Not Gif‚Äù, trying to distance itself from the inferior .GIF format. It‚Äôs now known to stand for ‚ÄúPortable Network Graphics‚Äù.

There‚Äôs a lot more technical information about .PNGs if you care to read about it, but let‚Äôs move on to something more immediately useful.

Living in a Material World

Now that the basics of image transparency are out of the way, we can talk about how we can use transparent images to enhance the materials and textures of our 3d elements.

One of the most useful techniques for transparency is to use a texture as a substitute for a bunch of geometry that would normally be extremely time-consuming and difficult to model. For example, imagine a scene where you need something with lots of holes in it, like a chain.

If you take a purist‚Äôs approach, you might try modeling it. Since it‚Äôs a repeating pattern, it‚Äôs not that difficult once you understand you can make one chain link and duplicate it. However, the sheer number of triangles you‚Äôd need to represent a whole chain of links could quickly blow your entire geometry budget.

Of course, you could definitely improve it by simplifying each link to have fewer triangles, but no matter how low you manage to get them, it‚Äôs still going to be a ton of triangles when duplicated that many times.

Don‚Äôt make it‚Äìfake it.

Don‚Äôt throw away that model just yet though. We can use a model like this to generate a texture that could be used to ‚Äòfake‚Äô the look of a chain. I‚Äôm going to use Blender to render out an image of the chain (or at least part of the chain, since it repeats). That new texture could be applied to much simpler geometry and most people will never even notice.

Without going through every step, since you might be using some other software, I‚Äôll explain the basic idea.

I set up a new camera and point it toward the model where I want to capture an image. In my case, I point it directly downward and move it into a position where it‚Äôs capturing the smallest amount of the chain that I need for the image to repeat. Opening a second 3d view and looking through the camera is important for proper alignment.

Another important step is making sure the camera is set to ‚ÄòOrthographic‚Äô. This will ensure that the captured image doesn‚Äôt contain any perspective. We need this image to appear flat so that it repeats seamlessly. With the camera selected, in the Object Data Properties tab, you can find a dropdown to change the camera from the default ‚ÄòPerspective‚Äô to ‚ÄòOrthographic‚Äô. You‚Äôll also need to go to the Output Properties tab and set the Resolution X & Y to values that will make the image square. You‚Äôll want powers of 2 here, like 128, 256, 512, 1024, etc. I‚Äôm making this texture pretty small and light, so 256 by 256 will work just fine.

A couple more things to check. You may find you need to adjust the camera‚Äôs ‚ÄòOrthographic Scale‚Äô so that we're not rendering too large an area. The most important thing, however, (and the whole point of this exercise) is to make sure that the rendered image comes out with a transparent background. This will vary from app to app, but in Blender, the parameter you‚Äôre looking for is in the Render Properties tab under ‚ÄòFilm‚Äô. It‚Äôs called ‚ÄòTransparent‚Äô.

Now when we render what the camera sees, (Render‚ÜíRender Image), we get an image of part of the chain with a transparent background.

Notice when we show the alpha channel by itself, we can clearly see that the chain parts are white (opaque) and the background remains black (transparent). This is exactly what we need.

üí°
If you‚Äôre thinking, ‚ÄúCouldn‚Äôt I just draw something like this in Photoshop or Krita or whatever?‚Äù, the answer is yes, absolutely. The advantage of this method is that we can easily try different views or zoom levels just by moving the camera around. Plus, the render will respect partially transparent things and render the alpha as various levels of gray.

So now we can save that rendered image and use it as a texture. Let‚Äôs make sure that we save it as a .PNG so we don‚Äôt discard the alpha transparency. You can save right from the rendered image window using ‚ÄòImage‚ÜíSave As‚Ä¶‚Äô. Note the options that appear for saving RGBA. That A is for the alpha channel and we definitely need it.

Now that we‚Äôre ready to apply this newly rendered texture, we can go ahead and hide all that high-polygon chain and camera stuff. I wouldn‚Äôt recommend deleting it entirely‚Äìwe may still need to revisit it for future work. In Blender, I make a habit of putting my ‚Äòsetup‚Äô stuff in its own Collection so I can keep things organized. Use whatever organization method(s) you like; a little extra time cleaning things up is always a good idea and you‚Äôll thank yourself later.

Alpha Clip vs. Alpha Blend

Another important part of understanding transparency is knowing the difference between Alpha Clip and Alpha Blend modes. We‚Äôve seen Alpha Clip in action with our chain. Alpha Clip is used when you need pure opacity and pure transparency. In other words, a pixel can either be ON or OFF. There is no in-between. As a result, Alpha Clip is super cheap in terms of rendering cost. The renderer simply discards any pixels below a certain threshold. You may have noticed that Blender has a slider to adjust this threshold. It‚Äôs in the same place as where you set the Blend Mode itself.

üí°
In some software, Alpha Clip is sometimes referred to as Alpha Test or Stencil Alpha. Regardless of the name, the tech behind it is usually the same.

The other type we can use in Hubs is called Alpha Blend. I say this with some trepidation because not only is Alpha Blend trickier to work with, it‚Äôs also more expensive to render.

Alpha Blend looks at the entire range of values in your Alpha texture and applies the corresponding amount of transparency. So, for example, black and white pixels are still ON or OFF, but in-between values (shades of gray) are semi-transparent. This is how you can achieve the look of things like glass or clear plastics.

The same material set to Alpha Blend achieves the look of semi-transparency since that mode can display the full range of alpha channel values.

But that nice soft transparency has its downsides too. Take a look at what happens when you have two surfaces with Alpha Blend on them. As soon as they overlap, depending on their distance, renderers can get confused about which one is in front.

This doesn‚Äôt necessarily mean you should never use Alpha Blend. But it‚Äôs good to be aware of the potential problems you might run into, especially if you know you intend to have multiple semi-transparent surfaces that will overlap when viewed at certain angles. This is often referred to as an ‚Äòalpha sorting problem‚Äô. The renderer doesn‚Äôt know what order to sort each surface so it can‚Äôt render them consistently.

This can also be seen when using no alpha texture at all but setting the Alpha slider to an in-between value. Alpha Blend will make the surfaces semi-transparent, but renderers can have a heck of a time trying to figure out what faces are in front.

But if you proceed with caution, and think about your scene as a whole, you can still use Alpha Blend without too much trouble. For example, in this scene, the window is the only item that has semi-transparency (alpha blend) so there are no issues with sorting to worry about.

So if you come away from this tutorial with only one thing, it should be that Alpha Clip is cheap and worry-free, whereas Alpha Blend can be slightly more expensive and may have sorting issues. Fortunately, you now know a few different ways to work around the problem.
